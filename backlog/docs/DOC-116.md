### Why DOC-094 didn't address this

DOC-094 was scoped to the **duplicate** `id-128d` anchor on the two alerting H3s — both pointed to the same ID, so HTML allowed only one to resolve and inbound links silently landed on the wrong section. The other shadow anchors are unique (each has its own ID); they don't break inbound links today. They're sub-optimal because:
- Slug-based anchors (e.g. `#search-and-filtering`, `#data-quality-dashboard`) are the canonical Features.md pattern for non-legacy headings (per DOC-094's rationale and the GitBook authoring rule).
- Shadow anchors are opaque to readers (a URL like `Features.md#id-7948` carries no semantic information; `Features.md#data-quality-dashboard` does).
- When a heading is renamed in the GitBook editor, the shadow anchor often survives but the slug changes — creating future duplication risk (the same class as DOC-094 caught for `id-128d`).

## Failure shape

**A4 (reference integrity)** — the in-page TOC and outbound inbound links use shadow anchors instead of canonical slug-based anchors. (Per `pillars/documentation/quality-framework.md`.)

## Context

- **Why low (not medium)**: the shadow anchors work today (no broken inbound links since they're unique IDs); the cosmetic / future-fragility concern is real but not user-impacting.
- **Why log at all**: per the world-class doc mission, opaque shadow anchors are sub-optimal compared to semantic slug anchors. The next time a heading is renamed in the GitBook editor, the shadow anchor will survive but the heading text will change — exactly the failure mode DOC-094 caught for `id-128d`. Sweeping now prevents future duplications.
- **Recurring pattern**: every page authored / migrated through the GitBook editor accumulates shadow anchors. Worth a Gate-7 extension: every page audit checks for shadow-anchor leakage and either preserves them (if inbound links rely on them) or drops them.
